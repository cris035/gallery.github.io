Aperture

A small, circular opening inside the lens that can change in diameter to control the amount of light reaching the camera's sensor as a picture is taken.

Remember

The LARGER the F-stop number, the smaller the opening of the Aperture; The SMALLER the F-stop number, the bigger the opening of the Aperture.

What is an f-stop? • The designation of lens aperture settings; also, the camera control that determines f-stop. The standard series is: f/1, f/1.4, f/2, f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8, f/11, f/16, f/22, f/32

Depth of Field

As the aperture of the lens is progressively reduced in size, subjects in front and behind the point of focus begin to appear sharp.

The widest apertures (f2, f4) give the least depth of field.
The smallest apertures (f16, f22) give the greatest depth of field.

Shutter Speed

What is it?

The length of time during which light will pass through a lens when the shutter is released. The standard shutter speeds are 1 second and the following fractions of a second: ½ , ¼, 1/8, 1/15, 1/30, 1/60, 1/125, 1/250, 1/500, 1/1000. These speeds are indicated on the control as: 1, 2, 4, 8, 15, 30, 60, 125, 250, 500, and 1000.

Tip

A shutter speed setting for a bright, sunny day - using an aperture of F11 - might be 1/125 second. A cloudy day might use 1/60 second with the same aperture, exposing the film to light for a longer period of time.

Remember

A fast shutter speed will use a larger aperture to avoid an under-exposed image. A slow-shutter speed requires a small aperture to avoid over-exposure.
